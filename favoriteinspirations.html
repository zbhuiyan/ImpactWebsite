Team Impact’s Favorite Inspirational Systems

  1. Picniic App (Family Organization Tool)

 Picniic is a family organization app that handles a variety of information, such as extracurricular activity schedules and grocery lists. There’s a few really nice things about the way this app is designed which we could you for our app. First off, the tile interface is a perfect ‘home screen’ for apps which have a lot of varied information and tasks to present - it is clear and concise without being overwhelming, and communicates the separate nature of each item. This is an interface that is readily applicable to our app, as it would allow us to display a lot of disparate options all at once in a clean manner.  Additionally, I really like the collaborative interface that allows for interactions like shared events and grocery lists through linked accounts. I can see how we could use a similar interface to coordinate efforts between couples or roommates - for example, to make sure that they don’t both end up purchasing a sofa when they only needed one. Additionally, social features could help users meet each other and make friends within a new city. Picniic is not without flaws, however:  I do not feel that the top bar with buttons for each family member is particularly clear. It feels like something will happen when a person’s button gets pushed (rather than getting an array of choices, which is the expected behavior for a side menu) - I think this designed to be a ‘sort’ function, but I still feel a traditional menu would be less confusing while yielding the same functionality.  Overall, this app offers lessons in making it easy for users to select a task and coordinate it with others, both of which are features that our app will benefit from, and also a lesson in not reinventing the wheel - if you are using an interaction paradigm that your users have seen before, don’t arbitrarily change what it does or how it works! (nominated and written up by Becca).

2) Bored Panda Lifehacks Article (https://www.boredpanda.com/life-hacks/)

There were some aspects about this webpage/system that we considered to be inspirational and would like to see in our own system. One was the collected information to be shared with those willing to learn new things that may help in their daily lives. Although this specific webpage only shared a relatively small list of interesting and helpful tips (collected from all over the Interwebs), it was similar to a database of useful information. In our own system, it would be ideal to have a database of similar useful information that our users would find value in. There were also some “bad” elements of this website that we would try and avoid. Namely, we would like to present our information in text followed by pictures, rather than pictures containing text. We would also like to categorize our “life hacks” rather than throw a bunch of random ones together that we assumed the user would appreciate. (Nominated and written up by Zarin)

3) Banyan Trees

Banyan trees are huge, sprawling trees with multiple trunks that grow throughout the Pacific. They support tons of birds; walking under a banyan can be deafening at times. The interconnected roots and sheer abundance of life found in these trees are a fantastic reminder of how with the right support, you can have something truly beautiful. I thought of this tree when we were looking for inspiration for our team because we want to help people build their lives. Starting out on your own is daunting, especially when you have no clue how to pay a bill, buy a car, find a place to live, etc. If our interface is designed well enough, hopefully we can provide support that allows people building their lives to flourish.

(Nominated and written up by Celina)

4) eHow Website

This website is refreshingly elegant. It has an interface for selecting categories within the user can further narrow down their search. One of the main questions to ask ourselves is how many categories we want? Too many is overwhelming, too few is bad. Having only a 3x3 grid seems like a reasonable choice in the case of eHow. We will need considerable domain knowledge to choose our categories wisely and sparingly. Furthermore, even the layout of the categories matters. A 9x1 list will provide a different experience from a 3x3 grid, and this difference is worth exploring.

Another great feature is the wall of trending articles. This keeps the website fresh for recurrent visitors such that they are not always greeted by a static page. For an app, this means the difference between being engaging versus feeling like an old dictionary.

A third inspirational feature is the incorporation of social media into the site. Users can vote their favorite articles, and these votes are featured on the trending articles. This gamifies the experience, generates traffic, and plays off of our desires to express ourselves. Some visual indication of social media seems valuable. (Nominated and written up by David)

5) Turbotax - Reduces a huge system down to simple questions to figure out what’s important

TurboTax has to design for users that won't get used to their product, since they will only use it once a year at tax time. In addition, changing circumstances mean that the users will likely have different needs each time they use the software, and that very few users will be "standard users". (some of their recent design decisions are explained here).

Since our target group is people establishing themselves in a new place, we will need to pay attention to the same considerations - most people don't move so often that it becomes totally mundane, so we need to be intuitive for those totally unfamiliar with our interface. We will also need to filter information in the same way, since the things you need in a new place are heavily dependent on what the new area is like (urban vs. rural, hot vs. cold, coastal vs. midwest). In short, we need to present a large amount of potentially novel information, without allowing it to become overwhelming.

(Nominated and written up by Sean)
