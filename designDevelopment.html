We reflected on the strengths and shortcomings of this design. Some of the shortcomings we found were the lack of centralized style or theme in the layout, too much overlap between pages (it felt repetitive), and confusing titles in the navigation panel.

Some of the strengths of our design were the gamified experience, customizability through the profile page, easy navigation, and the depth of content

We addressed these issues in our second iteration. Here, our focus was to standardize the style. We liked the idea of gamifying the app by having a house that fills in as users complete tasks, and we moved the house’s location (from its own tab to the homepage) so the app experience was more centralized. We also eliminated the overlap between pages by narrowing everything down to only 4 pages.

How does it work?  Build a paper prototype.

Upon opening the app, the user is greeted with the main page with the partially filled in house. We believe this page will gamify the experience and incentivize users to complete tasks. It also creates a central goal for the user. A possible downside of this is that some users might prefer to immediately see the list of tasks. We hope to explore this question through more user testing in the next phase.

The navigation bar is always visible up top. We narrowed the number of tabs down to 3, with an additional help button. We had the most design decisions to make as we were working on the tasks page. We drew inspiration from existing calendar apps and other task lists as shown in our portfolio of design ideas. We decided to ditch our horizontal timeline idea and instead opted for a more traditional, top to bottom chronological task list. This is layout is almost universally understood, but may not be the most aesthetically pleasing experience. Users can tap tasks to open up a detailed description. This description is a large pop up with a back button.

Finally, there is a settings tab where users can reset their profile and toggle general settings such as font size. The focus is on the profile setup here and we are not so concerned with the settings page. We want to create an intuitive and quick form that only asks relevant questions, much like TurboTax. We will focus more on the profile setup in the next phase.

Use your paper prototype to test this design idea with appropriate users. 

We tested our paper prototype with three users. All three were college aged students, so we will focus on having a greater diversity of testers in the next phase. All three students gave similar feedback. Namely, they each mentioned that we should include more integration between the tabs, and more buttons allowing them to know intuitively where to click next. They also requested confirmation that their profile data has been stored.

What is your design?

We have a web-app that provides the user with tasks relevant to moving to a new place. We are also providing a gamified experience for the users to want to complete these tasks.

How well does it meet user needs?  In what ways does it fall short?

It allows users to find the tasks they need to do in order to have a smooth transition to a new home/environment.

It may not be an extensive set of tasks at this point that are relevant to any user.

We are missing some elements that might make usage simpler and more intuitive (i.e. button leading to the next task from the homepage). Users also want confirmation that their data has been stored.

What important alternative designs did you consider, either initially or during the refinement of this idea?   Why did you reject them/prefer the one you chose?  

We considered creating a timeline earlier in the refinement of our idea, but we realized it was probably unnecessary and repetitive (because we already have a task list), plus it would easily get cluttered. The only benefit to having a timeline as well as our task list would be to further gamify the experience and to make the experience more unique.

What key insights did you gain in trying to produce a design that addresses your brief?

We learned that it would be unreasonable and over-scoped to try and tackle all possible solutions that might help our users. We decided that we should go with helping them on a higher level - helping them figure out what things they might need to do rather than helping them do the things they might need to do. We are a lot more satisfied with the scope of our project now that we have refined our design down.
