Formal Usability Study

        We ended up deviating from our proposed formal usability study. Rather than creating two prototypes with just a timeline and just a calendar, as proposed, we created two prototypes that had both the task list and one of either the timeline or the calendar. This still allowed us to compare the timeline and calendar, as well as get an understanding of how the two compare to a simple task list. It likely would have been better to create three entirely different prototypes and test with more people, but we did not find the time for this.

        The main takeaway from this formal usability study was that the task list is actually the faster and preferred method of completing tasks. The task list took an average of 2 clicks and 5-10 seconds whereas both the timeline and calendar view took ~15-20 seconds and 3-4 clicks. During the tests, both users landed on the task list first and immediately found the task they were told to complete. It took them significantly longer  to stumble upon the calendar and timeline views. This could potentially be due to a lack of clear signifiers in our website. For example, the calendar/timeline tab is on the right side of the navigation bar, and thus might not be as visible as the task list. However, even when they did stumble on these two pages, they were more confused than they were with the task list. Leon failed to realize you had to switch weeks in the calendar to find the required task. Leon and Will both complained about the lack of visibility of the challenges in these two views compared to the task list.

        Considering our limited time, our desire to keep the website minimalist, and the strong feedback, we decided not to include either the timeline or the calendar in our final design. We removed the page in the navigation bar. We hope this will streamline the experience.

Other notes:

        Will would have preferred to see a separate tab in the navigation. In this tab, he would have liked to see the option to toggle between the timeline and calendar view. He found both helpful, but leaned towards the timeline view.

        Also note that we could have done a much better job collecting quantitative data. We were overly focused on the testers’ narrative and thus got distracted from counting and measuring time. The times listed above are thus just estimates, but should still give an insight into the effectiveness.
