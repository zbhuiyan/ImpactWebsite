UX Design Final Report

-- Team Impact

When we first set out to develop an app, we sat down to discuss what might be interesting problems to solve. We thought about how productivity can be hindered by internet distractions; how Craigslist is not as streamlined as it could be, how habits can be difficult to make or break, how remembering things is difficult for everyone, and how moving is a long, drawn-out process for which there is often little support. The last problem seemed the most intriguing to us, especially considering that many college students end up moving to a brand-new place after they graduate, and that experience can be daunting, to say the least. In order to improve upon that potentially isolating experience, we decided to develop an app that would help young adults moving to unfamiliar places. Ideally, we wanted to make sure that any young adult could easily move without missing important steps or feeling lost along the way.


Our app, Reloca, is a personalized task reminder designed specifically with moving in mind. Tasks related to moving are suggested chronologically and users are encouraged to complete them for an in-app reward. 


We developed three user personas after having conducted interviews with young adults who either were in the process of moving, were looking to move, or had moved recently. Those personas then informed our design process, especially in the initial stages when we were figuring out what our app would provide and feel like. More information on the specifics of our personas can be found at https://drive.google.com/open?id=1guzvbv_NojtcWYPN_lmiMe5Kwc-c0OCB6aM-rtJYBYg.


Once we had developed our personas, there were a number of ways that we could proceed. An app to help our users move could take many forms, ranging from a simple check list for taking notes and scheduling tasks through an exhaustive list that includes every step of the moving process. Neither of those extremes were likely to be particularly useful to our users; a check list can function outside of a dedicated “moving app,” and our personas identify people who have or are looking for autonomy, rather than a micro-managing digital assistant. Additionally, extraneous complexity can significantly bog down both development and end-user interaction - the simpler you can make an app with a given feature set, the better.

 

With these concepts in mind, we chose to create a middle-ground app which provides auto-generated tasks and light guidance. Suggestions are tailored to the user’s needs through the collection of data such as current location, move date, and final destination.


User Experience

Following the first round of usability tests, we decided to add a new component to our app: a house, meant to help the user visually gauge their moving progress. The house starts out as just a frame, and new components are added anytime the user completes a task. By the end of the moving experience, a full house will have been constructed in the house view. This was a completely new idea that we came up with when we realized that our app needed something to more effectively engage the user.

This decision was driven by our usability tests. We received feedback that the app felt disjointed, and that there wasn’t anything pulling the pieces of the app together to make it seem like a cohesive system. The lack of a unified focal point made using the app feel dull and tedious - which, of course, we wanted to avoid. Also important in this decision was the fact that multiple people (not necessarily just through usability tests, but also through feedback in discussions we had about the scope of our project) mentioned the idea of “just a task list.” Our app was meant to streamline the process of moving, and we decided that outlining tasks would be the most useful way to do that. However, as we mentioned earlier, we did not want the app to feel like “just a task list” that could be replaced by any other system in which the user can keep track of their efforts. By adding in the house and completion-based functionality, we introduced a more creative element to our app that became the focal point we so needed. 


Task List

Throughout our design process, we debated the format that tasks should be presented in. This is an integral part of the experience and we wanted to maximize efficiency and visibility. We ended up designing a minimalist, chronological task list, chosen from a set of three proposed designs. The other two considerations were a timeline and calendar format, as shown below.

We conducted a formal usability study to help us quantify the experience. Based on our conducted study, the task list seemed to be the most efficient and most preferred method. It took users an average of 2 clicks and approximately 5-10 seconds to find and complete a specific task whereas the timeline and calendar views each took approximately 15-20 seconds and 3-4 clicks. This study confirmed that the task list is the most streamlined approach, as we had thought. We suspect that the large boxes and fonts improved visibility and decreased chances of overwhelming our users. Despite some feedback suggesting we add a “zoom” feature that allows users to adjust how many tasks they can see, we stuck with our design and showed a few tasks at a time, as we’d decided to try and keep from overwhelming the average user. More information on what went into our formal evaluation and specific choices can be found at http://hfid.olin.edu/sa2018/impact/FormalUsabilityTestResults.html.







User Flow

Making sure that our app had consistent user flow was very important in our design decision making. We received feedback from our heuristic evaluations, cognitive walkthroughs and usability tests suggesting a number of different ways we could improve user flow. Based on user feedback (largely from the heuristic evaluations), we streamlined the flow of the app experience by improving navigation, adding universal back functionality, and reducing button ambiguity. We found that users were often getting stuck on pages, especially in the task pop-ups. We noted this, and also received feedback that we had a lack of back buttons. They would try to circumvent this by returning to the home page and starting again, which was inefficient and frustrating. To fix this, we added a “back” button almost universally across pages. We also decreased ambiguity in button labels and added highlighting in the main navigation bar to signify which tab a user is currently on. Furthermore, we added customization as per the request of our testers. Users can now add their own custom tasks with specific due dates, as well as save tasks for later rather than having to complete them after clicking on the task. We also received feedback from users during our cognitive walkthroughs suggesting that we add some way for the user to be confident that we were saving their profile data. We decided to add a “Save!” button below the profile data entry boxes to allow the user some sort of confirmation that their data was being saved. More information about our heuristic evaluation can be found at http://hfid.olin.edu/sa2018/impact/FormalUsabilityTestResults.html.
