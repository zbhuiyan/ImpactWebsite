1. Using the synthesized feedback you collected in your Design Development, produce an implementation plan for a free-standing (i.e., no "wizard of Oz") automated prototype. Render this prototype in a series of design documents including a site-map or interaction flow and wireframes or other screen representations.

We revisited our prototyping priorities, which were listed in the last phase as:

  1. Task Interaction
  2. House Building
  3. Navigation

From there, we developed a plan for exactly what we wanted to implement in each part of our app. The features to be included in a minimum viable product are:

  • Task Interaction
  • View list of tasks in time-order (“Today”, “Tomorrow”, “This week”....)
  • Complete tasks by checking them off
  • Click/tap on tasks to open a zoom-in screen with more info about the task, and another way to indicate completion
  • Completion will result in the option to open the house view and will gray out the task.
  • House
  • Individual items (windows, doors, chimney)  are added to the “house” as the user earns them by completing tasks
  • Mouseover/tool tip shows when the user earned the component, what they did to earn it
  • Navigation
  • There should be tabs/links to house, tasks, settings/profile, access each page.
  • When a task is completed, there should be an option to go to the house view.
  • Back arrow or “close x” from task screen
  • Profile
  • Questions for the user that help us specify the tasks and help us give time estimates for the tasks, with confirmation that their data has been saved.
  • Settings (i.e. colorblind mode and text size)
  • Sidebar
  • Will include links to Profile/Settings, House,  Tasks, Help
  • Help
  • Includes basic instructions for using our website

In the last phase, we made some very deliberate choices about the structure and flow of our app, and this is represented in our plan. For instance, we decided to condense down from our original designs - there aren’t many different pages to focus on because we realized that having too many disjointed pages would not be helpful to us in the long run. We also realized that trying to generate a Help page, a Settings page, and a Profile page all on our own would likely just be a waste of time - rather than spending all our time on developing commonly used app elements, our plan is to use whatever we can find and modify it until it fits our needs. We’re creating something new overall, but there’s no reason not to use whatever tools we have available to us.

  1. Conduct a cognitive walkthrough of your prototype design, "proofreading" and resolving any ambiguities you are able to identify.

First, we decided what tasks we wanted to see a user complete in our cognitive walkthrough. When we designed our usability test in the previous phase, we came up with tasks in the spirit of preparing for a cognitive walkthrough. In this phase, we made those tasks more explicit, with a defined vision for what the user would do (i.e. rather than just saying “Complete a task,” we decided that “Complete a task having read the full instructions” would more effectively get users to open up the task description before completing it).

  • Complete your profile using your personal information.
  • Try changing your settings.
  • Complete a task having read the full instructions.
  • View your home, and find details about each piece.
  • Rearrange tasks according to what order you prefer doing them in.

[Questions to keep in mind for us]  

  1. Will the user try and achieve the right outcome?
  2. Will the user notice that the correct action is available to them?
  3. Will the user associate the correct action with the outcome they expect to achieve?
  4. If the correct action is performed; will the user see that progress is being made towards their intended outcome?

We also conducted a cognitive walkthrough and took notes on what our practice user had to say. That feedback was incorporated into our refinement process when we made a list of things we wanted to change about our prototype. Those changes are outlined below.

  1. What changed (especially: between paper prototyping and the proposed final design), and why did you change it? [Provide evidence/grounding.]

Moving from a paper prototype to a Figma prototype, we made a few changes on our own; namely, we expanded the navigation bar and added some branding (Reloca) to lend a more consistent feel across the interface. We also made some changes based on conducting cognitive walkthroughs with our paper prototype. These changes include adding things that would make the experience more intuitive for the user, such as the addition of buttons leading from one page to another.

The majority of this phase’s improvements came from a later cognitive walkthrough and our heuristic evaluation. After receiving feedback in these contexts, we added greater customizability for users, including the ability to mark tasks as “do later” and the ability to add custom tasks. Furthermore, we added back buttons so that users could undo mistakes.

  1. What (if any) key insights did you gain during this phase? [What evidence supports these insights?]

The scope of our prototype might not be as expanded as the scope of other teams’ but we realized that it’s more important to focus on the functionality and usefulness of our app than making it more complex.

  1. What questions do you have now about your project (i.e., are there things you would like to investigate)? What shortcomings are you aware of?

The heuristic evaluations by Team Haettenschweiler provided additional feedback which we used to improve our digital prototype. We are emphasizing resolving Severity 3 and 4 problems in our interface. Questions we’re beginning to address include:

  • How can we clarify the purpose of the app without explicit directions and too much text?
  • How can we add customizability elegantly?
  • How do we re-incorporate a timeline or calendar view without cluttering the app?
